In February 2020, as BP announced it would reinvent itself as a net zero company, ExxonMobil's then-CEO Darren Woods publicly dismissed net zero targets as "a beauty competition." The company has since published a Scope 1+2 net zero commitment for operated assets by 2050 — but has refused to set any target covering Scope 3 emissions.

"The IEA's net zero scenario is not our base case."
— ExxonMobil, CDP Response 2023

Scope 3 — the emissions produced when customers burn ExxonMobil's products — represents approximately 85–90% of total lifecycle emissions. A commitment covering only Scope 1+2 addresses roughly 10–15 cents of every emissions dollar the company is responsible for causing.

The numbers in this report are drawn entirely from ExxonMobil's own public disclosures — their CDP 2023 Climate Response, SEC Form 10-K 2024, and their Corporate Sustainability Report 2024. No independent modelling. No estimates.

In 2024, ExxonMobil completed the acquisition of Pioneer Natural Resources for approximately $60 billion — the largest oil and gas acquisition in decades. This added significant Permian Basin production capacity and locked in fossil fuel infrastructure for decades. The capital allocation is the strategy.

Key Findings
Finding 01
The pledge that does not exist.
Every company in the CONSCIENCE portfolio has published at least a net zero target — including BP, which subsequently abandoned its production reduction goal. ExxonMobil has not committed to net zero inclusive of Scope 3. When 85–90% of lifecycle emissions come from customers burning your product, a Scope 1+2 only commitment covers approximately 10–15% of the problem the company is responsible for causing. There is no pledge to verify here. There is only an absence of one.
Finding 02
$60 billion in the wrong direction.
In 2024, ExxonMobil completed the acquisition of Pioneer Natural Resources for approximately $60 billion — the largest oil and gas acquisition in decades. This added significant Permian Basin production capacity and locked in fossil fuel infrastructure for decades ahead. Companies on a credible net zero pathway do not make $60 billion bets on fossil fuel expansion. The capital allocation is the strategy.
Finding 03
Intensity vs absolute — the hidden retreat.
ExxonMobil reports emissions intensity reductions, not absolute reductions. Intensity metrics can fall while absolute emissions rise if production volume increases. With Pioneer adding significant output, absolute Scope 1+2 emissions are increasing even as intensity metrics may improve. VYRION measures absolute emissions against Paris Agreement pathways. Intensity is not the metric that determines 1.5°C outcomes.
Finding 04
Low carbon investment: 5 cents on every dollar.
ExxonMobil has committed $17 billion toward lower-carbon solutions through 2027. Total capital expenditure runs approximately $59 billion over the same period. That is approximately 5 cents of every capital dollar directed toward the energy transition. IEA net zero pathways require no new fossil fuel development after 2021 and a majority of investment in clean energy by 2030. The gap is not incremental. It is structural.
